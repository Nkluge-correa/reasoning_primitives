\begin{abstract}
We study reasoning through two primitives---recall and state-tracking---and ask whether hybrid architectures combining attention-based retrieval with recurrent state updates outperform attention-only models on tasks requiring both. Using matched OLMo3 transformer and hybrid models in instruction-tuned and reasoning-augmented variants, we evaluate five controlled task families. Reasoning augmentation provides the largest overall improvement, but its benefit has a ceiling: at high difficulty, the hybrid Think model remains robust on tasks with sequentially chained state updates, while the transformer Think model is more robust on tasks with flat multi-hop retrieval. Parse rate figures reveal a further dimension---the transformer Think model's collapse on the collision task is a generation breakdown, not merely an accuracy failure, while the hybrid Think model on Dyck maintains accuracy even as its parse rate degrades. We present these findings as suggestive rather than conclusive.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Chain-of-Thought reasoning substantially improves LLM performance on complex tasks \cite{wei2022chain}, and this picture has been extended across prompting strategies \cite{yao2023tree}, architectures \cite{geiping2025scaling}, and training regimes \cite{wang2025thinking,yang2025qwen3,bakouch2025smollm3,zeng2026glm}. The harder question is why. Levy et al.~\cite{levy2025state} propose the \textit{State over Tokens} (SoT) framework: because internal activations do not persist across generation steps, intermediate tokens serve as the only durable carrier of state. Reasoning tokens improve performance not by making the model explain itself, but by letting it externalize partial results and transform bounded computations into cumulative ones \cite{kambhampati2025reasoning,liu2025logical}.

This framing makes architecture central. Transformers excel at content-based retrieval but face well-known scaling constraints \cite{fichtl2025end,li2024survey}; recurrent and state-space architectures improve efficiency but sacrifice precise addressable retrieval \cite{yang2024parallelizing,yang2024gated,dao2024transformers}. Hybrid architectures combine both \cite{merrill2026olmo}. We ask: under what conditions do architectural differences become visible when reasoning is decomposed into its constituent computational demands?

\section{Primitives, Tasks, and Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Reasoning primitives.} Following Merrill et al.~\cite{merrill2026olmo}, we decompose reasoning into \textit{state-tracking}---maintaining and updating structured variables across sequential transformations---and \textit{recall}---retrieving specific information from a large context with high precision. When combined into \textbf{state-based recall}, the retrieval address is itself determined by a sequence of state transformations, making the task irreducible to either primitive alone (see Appendix~\ref{app:code-examples} for canonical examples).

\paragraph{Models.} We compare OLMo3-7B and OLMo3-Hybrid-7B \cite{merrill2026olmo}, a matched pair sharing the same data, optimization schedule, and training recipe, in both instruction-tuned and reasoning-augmented variants: \texttt{OLMo-3-7B-\{Instruct,Think\}} and \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-\{Instruct,Think\}-SFT-7B}. Inference used vLLM \cite{kwon2023efficient}; Think variants: temperature 0.0, max 6,000 tokens; Instruct variants: temperature 0.0, max 40 tokens.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} All tasks are free-form generation scored by exact matching. Difficulty is parameterised by $m$ (retrieval complexity) and $n$ (state maintenance complexity), with 1000 instances per $(m,n)$ bin except for the DYCK task which are only 500 instances per $(m,n)$. All tasks require JSON output \texttt{\{"answer": "A|B|C|D"\}}. We report \textit{accuracy} and \textit{parse rate} (fraction of parseable responses) separately, since the two can dissociate: at high difficulty, Think models can fail to produce any structured output---a generation breakdown distinct from an incorrect answer.

\paragraph{Tasks.} We evaluate five procedurally generated families (prompt examples in Appendix~\ref{app:task-examples}). \textbf{State-based Astro Recall} (\textit{astro}): $m$ real exoplanet rows in a Markdown table; variables initialized to orbital period values undergo $n$ simultaneous swaps; the model retrieves the planet name for the queried variable. \textbf{Collision Simulator} (\textit{collisions}): $m$ particles with distinct velocities undergo $n$ sequential elastic collisions (velocity exchange); each collision conditions all subsequent ones, creating compounding sequential dependencies. \textbf{OLMo Original} (\textit{olmo\_original}): minimal state-based recall from \citet{merrill2026olmo}---a bit array of length $m$, $m$ pointer variables, $n$ simultaneous swaps, evaluate \texttt{bits[a]}; replication baseline. \textbf{Dyck Language} (\textit{dyck}): bracket sequence with one masked closer; model maintains a stack to identify the correct token; $m$ = stack depth, $n$ = sequence length; no retrieval component. \textbf{DAG Arithmetic} (\textit{dag}): $m$ input variables, $n$ computation layers introducing new variables via addition/subtraction with arbitrary cross-layer references \cite{motwani2026longcot}; flat multi-hop retrieval over a growing registry.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Collision Simulator: Hybrid Advantage}

Under instruction-only prompting, both architectures hover between 0.27--0.40, near chance. The Think variants diverge sharply beyond $(16,16)$: \texttt{OLMo-3-7B-Think} falls to 0.49 at $(32,32)$ and collapses to near zero at $(64,64)$; \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-Think-SFT-7B} retains 0.94 and 0.75 at the same settings (Figure~\ref{fig:collisions_acc}). Figure~\ref{fig:collisions_parse} presents a contrasting trend, showing that the parse rate of \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-Think-SFT-7B} remains consistently below that of \texttt{OLMo-Think-SFT-7B}. However, this suggests that although \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-Think-SFT-7B} produces fewer successfully parsed outputs overall, the responses that are successfully parsed are more likely to contain the correct answer. In contrast to the transformer-based Think model, whose degradation is reflected in an increasing inability to generate parseable outputs. This behavior is consistent with the hypothesis that hybrid recurrence stabilizes intermediate state propagation over long sequential reasoning chains, thereby preserving solution quality even when output formatting deteriorates.

\subsection{DAG Arithmetic: Transformer Advantage}

Both Think models reach perfect accuracy through $(8,8)$. Beyond $(16,16)$ the pattern reverses: \texttt{OLMo-3-7B-Think} retains 0.56 at $(32,32)$ while \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-Think-SFT-7B} falls to 0.30 (Figure~\ref{fig:dag_acc}). Crucially, parse rates remain high for both models (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:dag_parse}), confirming that this gap reflects genuine computational failure rather than a generation breakdown. The flat, multi-hop retrieval structure of DAG arithmetic---where any prior variable may be referenced at any later layer---appears to favour the transformer's content-addressable attention over the hybrid's recurrent state updates.

\subsection{Remaining Tasks}

\textbf{Astro Recall.} Both Think models achieve $\approx$0.95--0.97 at $(4,4)$ but collapse to 0.00/0.24 by $(32,32)$; Instruct variants hover near chance throughout. The joint demand of simultaneous swaps and large-table lookup exceeds what any model sustains beyond moderate difficulty (Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:astro_acc},~\ref{fig:astro_parse}).

\textbf{Dyck Language.} \texttt{OLMo-Hybrid-Think-SFT-7B} achieves the highest accuracy in the study (peak 0.79, never below 0.38 at any setting); \texttt{OLMo-3-7B-Think} is substantially more volatile (peak 0.67, dip to 0.22). Both Instruct variants hover near the 0.33 baseline. Notably, the hybrid Think model's parse rate degrades at high difficulty while its accuracy remains comparatively high---suggesting its recurrent components support correct internal computation even when output structure fails (Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:dyck_acc},~\ref{fig:dyck_parse}).

\textbf{OLMo Original.} Both Think models begin near-perfect ($\approx$0.91--0.92) and degrade in parallel toward chance---no consistent architectural advantage at the Think level. At the Instruct level, an unexpected reversal: transformer Instruct outperforms hybrid Instruct (0.43--0.53 vs.\ 0.32--0.43) (Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:olmo_acc},~\ref{fig:olmo_parse}).

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

Two patterns are consistent across all five tasks. First, \textbf{reasoning augmentation is the dominant factor}: Think variants substantially outperform Instruct variants at low-to-moderate difficulty, consistent with the SoT account that externalizing state into tokens enables deeper cumulative computation. Second, \textbf{this mechanism has a ceiling}, and what fills the gap depends on task structure.

The collision and DAG arithmetic results establish this most precisely. On collisions---strictly chained updates where each step overwrites prior state---the hybrid Think model's recurrent components supplement token-externalized state when reasoning alone fails, and sustain generation where the transformer collapses entirely. On DAG arithmetic---flat multi-hop retrieval over a growing variable registry---the transformer's content-addressable attention proves more robust, and both models maintain high parse rates, confirming genuine computational rather than generation failure. The Dyck parse-rate/accuracy dissociation adds a further nuance: the hybrid model's recurrent components may support correct computation at a level not fully captured by structured-output scoring.

These results suggest \textbf{architectural inductive biases are task-specific affordances}: chained sequential updates favour hybrid recurrence; flat multi-hop retrieval favours transformer attention. The clearest methodological takeaway is that benchmarks for architectural evaluation should target regimes where reasoning traces are helpful but insufficient, stressing both chained and flat retrieval structures---and should report parse rate alongside accuracy to distinguish computational failures from generation breakdowns.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Model family scope.} All experiments use a single matched OLMo3 pair. Whether findings replicate across other hybrid architectures (Mamba, RWKV), other transformer families, or other scales remains open.

\textbf{Single parameter scale.} All models are evaluated at 7B. Relative advantages and difficulty thresholds are likely scale-dependent.

\textbf{Task coverage.} The five families probe recall, state-tracking, and their composition in synthetic settings. Performance here does not license claims about general reasoning ability.

\textbf{Zero-shot only.} It is not possible to determine how much of the observed gap reflects architectural differences versus distributional mismatch. Fine-tuning experiments would allow a cleaner test.
